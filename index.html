Australian Labour Market

Key Insights

The Australian labour market is a key driver of the nation's economic stability, representing diverse industries and employment sectors. Metrics such as unemployment rates and employment participation help understand workforce health.

Understanding labour market trends is critical for addressing economic challenges. This dashboard highlights employment and unemployment trends, participation rates, and workforce transitions across various regions of Australia.

The Rise of Skilled Workers

Australian Labour Market Trends (2020-2024)

Unemployment rates decreased significantly while employment-to-population ratios improved, highlighting a strong recovery across all states.

Year level slider to show changes in employment and unemployment rates over time for Australian states.

In 2020, Australian states like South Australia and Tasmania experienced higher unemployment rates (6%-8%) and lower employment-to-population ratios, but by 2024, there was a significant labour market recovery with lower unemployment and higher employment rates, especially in the Australian Capital Territory and Victoria.

Participation Rate by State in Australia

What is participation rate?
% of the working-age population that is either employed or actively seeking work.

There is a noticeable variation in participation rates, with states like the Northern Territory and Western Australia showing higher rates (above 70%).

This is unlike states like Tasmania and South Australia, which have lower participation rates (closer to 62-64%).

Tasmania has one of the lowest participation rates, with values close to 62%, as shown in black.

Employment in Australia Based On Skill Levels

The toggle allows users to select a specific skill level, which is then highlighted in the graph.

Global Financial Crisis (2008): Sharp decline in employment across all skill levels.

COVID-19 Pandemic (2020): A steep drop followed by a rapid recovery in employment.

The graph shows that employment for individuals with higher qualifications (Skill Level 1 - Bachelor degree or higher) has consistently been higher, peaking around 2011 and 2021, while lower skill levels (Skill Levels 4 and 5) exhibit more stable but lower employment, suggesting a labour market preference for higher-skilled workers.

Australian Labor Market Transitions: 2022 to 2023

What does this diagram mean?
This Sankey diagram shows workforce transitions in Australia from 2022 to 2023, highlighting how individuals moved between full-time, part-time employment, unemployment, and being out of the labor force.

The diagram reveals that there was significant flow from "unemployed in 2022" to "employed part-time/full-time in 2023," indicating a potential improvement in labour conditions.
