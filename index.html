Australian Labour Market

Target

The Australian labor market is a key driver of the nation's economic stability, representing diverse industries and employment sectors. Metrics such as unemployment rates and employment participation help understand workforce health.

Understanding labor market trends is critical for addressing economic challenges. This dashboard highlights employment and unemployment trends, participation rates, and workforce transitions across various regions of Australia.

Employment Trends

In 2020, most Australian states had higher unemployment rates (ranging between 6% and 8%) with lower employment-to-population ratios, particularly in states like South Australia and Tasmania.

By 2024, there is a noticeable decrease in unemployment rates across the board, with an increase in the employment-to-population ratio, indicating a significant labor market recovery, particularly in states such as the Australian Capital Territory and Victoria.

This map shows the labor force participation rate across different states in Australia, with Victoria having a participation rate of 68.1%.

There is a noticeable variation in participation rates, with states like the Northern Territory and Western Australia showing higher rates (above 70%) compared to states like Tasmania and South Australia, which have lower participation rates (closer to 62-64%).

The graph shows that employment for individuals with higher qualifications (Skill Level 1 - Bachelor degree or higher) has consistently remained higher compared to other skill levels, peaking notably around 2011 and 2021.

In contrast, lower skill levels (Skill Levels 4 and 5) exhibit more stable but lower employment numbers, with fewer fluctuations over the time period, indicating a possible preference in the labor market for higher-skilled workers.

Unemployment Trends

This graph shows the unemployment trends from 1991 to 2023 across several countries, highlighting fluctuations in unemployment rates over time.

Australia, indicated by the bold line, shows a consistent decline in unemployment after peaking in the early 1990s, with a sharp rise around the 2020 period likely reflecting the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, followed by a rapid recovery.
